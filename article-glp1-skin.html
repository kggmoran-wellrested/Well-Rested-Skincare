GLP-1 medications and your skin — what actually happens and how to respond

Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro — GLP-1 medications are changing how millions of women lose weight. They're also changing their skin. Here's the honest science, and what a dermatologist-informed routine actually looks like.

By Katherine
April 2026
9 min read

I've been on Noom's microdose program for about a year. The weight loss has been modest — that's the point of microdosing — but it's been real. And somewhere around month four or five, I started noticing something I hadn't expected: a subtle softening along my cheeks and jawline. Not dramatic. Not what you'd call gaunt. But the definition I was used to seeing was slightly less there, and the skin along my jaw had a quality I can only describe as early laxity.

I wasn't alarmed — I was curious. I knew enough about skin physiology to understand what was probably happening, and I immediately started thinking about how to respond to it intelligently. I added retinol and copper peptides to my routine consistently. I started using my ZIIP nanocurrent device and MyoLift microcurrent regularly. And eventually, I did a CO2 laser treatment, which made a meaningful difference. My skin now looks better than it did before I started noticing the laxity — because I responded to it proactively rather than waiting.

I'm writing this article because I couldn't find a genuinely useful skincare guide for women on GLP-1s when I went looking for one. What I found was either medical content about Ozempic face that reads like a warning label, or marketing copy for overpriced "GLP-1 skincare" products that aren't doing anything your existing routine couldn't do. Neither was what I actually needed. So here's the article I wanted to find.

What GLP-1 medications actually do

GLP-1 receptor agonists — semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy), tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound), and others — were originally developed to manage blood sugar in type 2 diabetes. They work by mimicking glucagon-like peptide-1, a naturally occurring hormone that regulates appetite and slows gastric emptying, producing a sustained feeling of fullness and significantly reduced calorie intake. The result is often substantial, relatively rapid weight loss.

The word "rapid" is the key one for skin. Your skin is remarkably adaptive — but it adapts on its own timeline, not yours. When the fat beneath it reduces faster than the skin can contract, you get laxity. This isn't unique to GLP-1 medications; anyone who loses weight quickly through any means can experience the same thing. But because GLP-1s are so effective and so widely used, dermatologists are now seeing it at scale.

The Research

A 2025 study from Vanderbilt University found approximately 9% midface volume loss for every 10kg of total weight loss in GLP-1 patients. The effect is most pronounced in the temples, cheeks, under-eye area, and jawline — exactly where facial fat provides the structural support that keeps skin looking lifted and taut.

The severity of skin changes depends heavily on two variables: how much weight is lost and how quickly it's lost. This is why microdosers like me tend to notice subtle, gradual changes rather than the dramatic transformation that gets talked about online. Someone on a full therapeutic dose losing 40+ pounds in six months will have a very different experience than someone on a low dose losing 10 pounds over a year. Neither experience is wrong — they just require different levels of response.

The negative effects

— and what's driving them

The term "Ozempic face" — coined by dermatologist Dr. Paul Frank — has become shorthand for a cluster of cosmetic changes that can accompany GLP-1-related weight loss. The term is a bit of a misnomer (it's not the medication causing the changes, it's the weight loss) but it accurately describes what patients are presenting with.

Most Common
Facial Volume Loss & Laxity

As subcutaneous fat reduces, the structural scaffolding beneath skin diminishes. Cheeks hollow, the jawline softens, and under-eye hollowing becomes more pronounced. Skin that previously had fat support beneath it now has to do more work on its own — and collagen density typically isn't adequate to compensate without intervention.

Often Overlooked
Collagen & Elastin Disruption

Recent research suggests GLP-1 medications may directly affect the stem cells responsible for collagen and elastin production — independent of the weight loss itself. This means the skin changes aren't purely mechanical. There may be a biological component that makes skin slower to rebound even when weight loss is gradual.

Body, Not Just Face
Skin Laxity Elsewhere

Volume loss isn't limited to the face. The neck, upper arms, abdomen, and thighs are commonly affected — particularly with more significant weight loss. "Ozempic body" is becoming as recognized a term as Ozempic face in aesthetic medicine circles.

For Some Users
Dryness & Sensitivity

Some users report increased skin dryness and sensitivity, potentially related to changes in nutrition, hydration habits, or the metabolic shifts accompanying GLP-1 use. Not universal — I personally didn't experience this — but worth monitoring, particularly if your diet becomes lower in healthy fats during weight loss.

The positive effects — the part nobody talks about

The conversation around GLP-1s and skin tends to focus entirely on the negative. But there are clinically documented benefits that deserve equal attention — and that actually make GLP-1 users good candidates for certain skincare ingredients.

Anti-Inflammatory
Reduced Systemic Inflammation

GLP-1 medications have demonstrated meaningful anti-inflammatory effects through cytokine modulation. For skin, this can translate to clearer, calmer complexions — and documented improvements in inflammatory conditions like psoriasis and hidradenitis suppurativa. If you've had stubborn redness or inflammation, you may notice it improving.

Metabolic Benefit
Better Glucose Regulation

Glucose spikes trigger a process called glycation, which damages collagen and accelerates aging. By moderating blood sugar, GLP-1s may actually slow one of the key mechanisms of skin aging. This is a genuine, science-backed benefit that most GLP-1 skin content completely ignores.

The skin changes from GLP-1 medications aren't a reason to avoid them — they're a reason to respond to them intelligently.

The skincare response

Related: at-home devices for laxity →

— what actually works

The good news is that the skincare response to GLP-1-related skin changes maps almost exactly to what a well-designed anti-aging routine already looks like. You don't need specialty "GLP-1 skincare" products — you need the right actives, deployed consistently, with some additional attention to the specific mechanisms at play.

Retinoids — non-negotiable

Retinoids remain the most clinically validated ingredient for collagen stimulation and skin cell turnover. For GLP-1 users, they're especially important because they directly address the collagen deficit that drives laxity. If you're not already using a retinoid consistently, now is the time to start. If you are, consider whether you're at a strong enough concentration to produce real results. Retinaldehyde — one step closer to retinoic acid than retinol — offers more efficacy with less irritation, and is worth considering if you've plateaued on standard retinol.

Copper peptides — the underrated essential

Copper peptides (specifically GHK-Cu, copper tripeptide-1) are carrier peptides that transport copper to skin cells — a critical cofactor for the enzymes responsible for collagen cross-linking and structural repair. A 1998 study found GHK-Cu increased collagen production by 70% after one month. For skin dealing with laxity and structural support loss, this class of ingredient is one of the most targeted tools available. I added copper peptides to my routine consistently after I noticed the changes in my jaw, and they've become a permanent fixture.

DNA repair technology

One of the most compelling newer ingredient categories for GLP-1 users is DNA repair technology — specifically the DNAzyme platform developed by Johns Hopkins researchers behind Plated Skin Science. GLP-1s may affect how skin cells repair and regenerate at a fundamental level, making ingredients that address cellular damage at the source (rather than just the surface) particularly relevant.

Microcurrent — the at-home lift

Microcurrent technology delivers low-level electrical impulses that stimulate the facial muscles and increase ATP production — the cellular energy currency that powers tissue repair. It's been used in professional esthetic settings for decades, and at-home devices have become genuinely effective. I use both the ZIIP nanocurrent device (for surface-level current and ingredient penetration) and the MyoLift for deeper muscular work. Consistency is everything with microcurrent — it's not a one-session fix, but used regularly it produces visible lifting and firming that's hard to replicate with topicals alone.

A Note on Dose

The severity of your skin response will track closely with the amount and speed of your weight loss. Microdosers typically see subtle, gradual changes that respond well to a consistent at-home routine. Patients on full therapeutic doses with significant weight loss may need professional intervention — fillers, biostimulatory injectables, or devices — to address more pronounced volume loss. Know where you are on that spectrum and respond accordingly.

Professional Treatment

When to see a dermatologist — CO2 laser

For more significant laxity — particularly in the lower face, jawline, and neck — at-home skincare and devices will only take you so far. CO2 laser resurfacing is one of the most effective professional treatments for skin tightening and collagen remodeling, and it's worth a serious conversation with your dermatologist if you're seeing changes that topicals and microcurrent aren't fully addressing. I had CO2 done and it produced a meaningful improvement. It's not a small undertaking — there is real downtime — but the results are substantive in a way that no serum can replicate.

The lifestyle layer

— don't skip this

Skincare alone won't be enough if the foundations aren't in place. For GLP-1 users, three lifestyle factors have direct, documented effects on skin quality during weight loss.

Protein intake. Collagen is a protein. If you're eating significantly less food — which GLP-1s are designed to produce — you need to be intentional about getting enough protein to support both muscle preservation and collagen synthesis. Most dermatologists recommend prioritizing protein as the first dietary consideration for GLP-1 patients concerned about skin.

Strength training. Building and maintaining muscle has a tightening effect on the skin above it. It also improves circulation, which directly benefits skin cell health and repair. If you're on a GLP-1 and not strength training, you're leaving one of the most effective tools against laxity on the table.

Hydration. Hyaluronic acid — the molecule responsible for water-binding capacity in skin — is affected by metabolic changes. Adequate hydration supports everything else you're doing, and it's one of the simplest interventions available.

The bottom line

GLP-1 medications are genuinely changing people's lives. For women in midlife — already navigating perimenopausal skin changes alongside the effects of weight loss — they can also change your skin in ways that deserve a thoughtful, proactive response.

The key insight is this: the severity of what you'll experience depends enormously on dose and rate of loss. Microdosers will likely see subtle, gradual changes that respond well to a consistent, well-chosen routine. Higher-dose users with more significant weight loss will need to think more seriously about professional intervention alongside their at-home work.

Either way, the ingredients and tools that address GLP-1 skin changes — retinoids, copper peptides, DNA repair technology, microcurrent — are the same ones that make up a genuinely effective anti-aging routine. You're not starting from scratch. You're just prioritizing more intentionally, and giving your skin what it needs to keep up with what your body is doing.
