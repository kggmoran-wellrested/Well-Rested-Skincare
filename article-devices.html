Editorial / At-Home Devices

Routine Guide

Your skin has a new power tool.
Here's how to use it.

At-home skincare devices have crossed a threshold. The technology that once lived exclusively in dermatology offices is now sitting on bathroom shelves — and at a fraction of the price. Here's what's worth it, what works, and how to build a device routine that actually fits your life.

Well Rested Editorial
March 2026
10 min read

Not long ago, if you wanted a microcurrent facial, you booked an appointment and paid $150 for 45 minutes. If you wanted laser resurfacing, you set aside a weekend for recovery. If you wanted LED light therapy, you sat under a machine at a med spa and hoped the esthetician wasn't running late.

That's changed. The at-home device market has matured significantly — not just in terms of price accessibility, but in actual clinical efficacy. Today's best devices use the same core technologies as professional treatments, simply calibrated for safe, consistent at-home use. The results aren't identical to clinical treatments, but they're real — and they compound beautifully over time.

This guide breaks down the four main device categories, explains the science in plain English, and helps you figure out which technology (or combination) is right for where your skin is right now.

"The best device routine isn't the most expensive one — it's the one you'll actually do consistently. Four minutes three times a week beats an hour once a month, every time."

Laser technology: collagen from within

Laser devices work by delivering controlled light energy into the dermis — the deeper layer of skin where collagen and elastin live. The energy creates a mild thermal effect that signals the body to kick its natural repair and collagen-production processes into gear. Non-fractional, non-ablative lasers (the at-home standard) do this without breaking the skin surface, meaning zero downtime.

Collagen loss starts in your mid-20s and accelerates with age. Topical ingredients like peptides and retinol can slow that decline from the outside — but laser is one of the few at-home technologies that stimulates new collagen production from within the dermis itself. The results build over 60–90 days of consistent use.

The Science

How at-home lasers stimulate collagen

Non-ablative laser energy at 1450nm targets the dermis without damaging the epidermis. The gentle heat activates fibroblasts — the cells responsible for producing collagen and elastin — triggering a wound-healing response that rebuilds the skin's structural matrix over time. Unlike collagen creams (whose molecules are too large to penetrate the skin barrier), laser energy works at the level where collagen is actually made.

Microcurrent: the facial workout

Microcurrent devices deliver low-level electrical currents that mimic the body's own bioelectrical signals. Think of it as a workout for your facial muscles — the current causes micro-contractions that, over time, lift, tone, and re-educate the muscles responsible for facial contour. You also get an immediate bonus: the boost in circulation and lymphatic drainage that gives you that "just had a facial" glow.

The difference between microcurrent and nanocurrent matters. Microcurrent (measured in microamps) targets muscle tissue for lifting and contouring — you feel this as a tingle and see results within hours. Nanocurrent (measured in nanoamps, 1000x smaller) works at the cellular level, stimulating collagen, elastin, and ATP production for longer-term skin health. The best devices use both.

Microneedling: controlled renewal

Microneedling — also called collagen induction therapy — uses a pen-shaped device with tiny needles to create controlled micro-channels in the skin. Your body responds to these micro-injuries by flooding the area with collagen and growth factors, accelerating cell turnover and skin renewal. It also dramatically increases the absorption of serums applied immediately after treatment.

At-home microneedling is most effective for texture, acne scarring, enlarged pores, and early signs of aging. It requires more preparation than other devices (clean skin, sterile cartridges, appropriate needle depth) but the results — particularly for scarring and texture — are hard to match with any topical alone.

LED & Light Therapy: the gentle powerhouse

LED (Light Emitting Diode) therapy uses specific wavelengths of light to trigger different biological responses in the skin. Red light (around 630–660nm) stimulates collagen production and reduces inflammation. Near-infrared (850nm) penetrates deeper to support firmer, healthier tissue. Blue light (415nm) targets acne-causing bacteria. Unlike lasers, LED emits no heat and requires no recovery — making it the most accessible device category for daily use.

The key variable between LED devices is whether they use continuous or pulsed light. Pulsed LED — where the light turns on and off in rapid bursts — has been shown in clinical studies to outperform continuous wave in two important ways: it optimizes ATP production at the cellular level, and it dramatically increases the absorption of skincare ingredients applied before treatment.

High-Frequency: the acne specialist

High-frequency devices pass a mild electrical current through a glass electrode filled with either neon or argon gas, generating ozone at the skin's surface. That ozone kills acne-causing bacteria, reduces inflammation, oxygenates the skin, and accelerates healing. It also improves circulation and boosts serum absorption — making it a smart first step before applying your actives.

High-frequency is one of the oldest technologies in professional skincare (it's been used in esthetician practices for over a century), which means the science is well-established and the at-home versions are very affordable. It's not a replacement for a full skincare routine, but as an add-on for acne-prone or congested skin, it punches well above its price point.

Building your device routine

You don't need all four technologies. Start with the one that matches your primary skin concern, build consistency with it for 90 days, then layer in a second if you want to. Here's how a multi-device routine might look — ordered from the most intensive to the most gentle:

1
Monthly — Microneedling
Every 4–6 weeks, on a dedicated evening. Cleanse thoroughly, use sterile cartridge, apply a hydrating serum immediately after. Plan for 24–48 hours of mild redness. Do not use actives for 7 days post-treatment.
→ Dr. Pen A6S
2
Daily (AM or PM) — Laser
2 minutes, every day. The easiest habit to build — the NIRA Pro can be used while doing something else. Works best on clean, moisturized skin. Long-term collagen investment that builds over 90 days.
→ NIRA Pro
3
3–5x Per Week — Microcurrent or LED
Choose based on your primary concern: microcurrent (ZIIP or MyoLift) for lifting and contouring, LED (Maysama PRANA) for collagen and glow. Apply a serum before LED to maximize absorption. Microcurrent needs a conductive gel.
→ ZIIP HALO or Maysama PRANA
4
Every Other Day — High Frequency (if acne-prone)
2–3 minutes on clean skin before applying serums. The ozone effect kills bacteria and boosts absorption of everything you apply next. Skip on microneedling days and on active-ingredient days to avoid over-stimulation.
→ NuDerma

A note on layering: don't use multiple devices in the same session. Each technology creates a response in the skin — stacking them increases the risk of irritation and doesn't improve results. Space them across your week intentionally, and always listen to your skin.
